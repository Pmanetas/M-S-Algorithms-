## üéØ CORE RATES (The Foundation)

### 1. **10-Year Treasury Yield**
**What it is:** The interest rate the US government pays on 10-year bonds.

**Why it matters:** 
- This is the "risk-free rate" - the baseline for all investing
- Everything else is compared to this
- When this goes up, borrowing gets more expensive everywhere

**How to interpret:**
- **2-3%:** Low rates, cheap money
- **3-4%:** Normal/moderate rates
- **4-5%:** Higher rates, expensive borrowing
- **5%+:** Very high rates

**Real-world impact:**
- Higher = mortgage rates go up
- Higher = stocks become less attractive (bonds compete)
- Higher = economy usually slows down

---

### 2. **2-Year Treasury Yield**
**What it is:** The interest rate on 2-year US government bonds.

**Why it matters:**
- Shows what markets expect for short-term rates
- More sensitive to Fed policy changes
- Compares to 10Y to show yield curve

**How to interpret:**
- **Compare to 10Y:** If 2Y > 10Y = inverted curve (recession warning!)
- **Compare to Fed Funds:** Shows market expectations

**Real-world impact:**
- Affects short-term lending rates
- Signals Fed policy expectations
- Inverted curve (2Y > 10Y) = recession usually follows in 6-18 months

---

### 3. **Fed Funds Rate**
**What it is:** The interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans. Set by the Federal Reserve.

**Why it matters:**
- **This is THE rate that controls everything**
- Fed raises this to fight inflation
- Fed lowers this to stimulate economy

**How to interpret:**
- **0-1%:** Emergency stimulus (2008, 2020)
- **1-3%:** Accommodative (easy money)
- **3-5%:** Neutral to restrictive
- **5%+:** Very restrictive (fighting inflation)

**Real-world impact:**
- Directly affects credit card rates
- Directly affects savings account rates
- Controls money supply in economy

---

## üí∞ RISK PREMIUMS (How Much Extra Return You Demand)

### 4. **Equity Risk Premium**
**What it is:** How much extra return you expect from stocks vs safe bonds.

**Formula:** Earnings Yield (from stocks) - 10-Year Treasury

**Why it matters:**
- **This tells you if stocks are cheap or expensive**
- Higher = stocks are attractive
- Lower = stocks are expensive

**How to interpret:**
- **> 4%:** Stocks very attractive (buy signal)
- **3-4%:** Stocks moderately attractive
- **2-3%:** Fair value
- **1-2%:** Stocks getting expensive
- **< 1%:** BUBBLE TERRITORY (danger!)

**Color coding on your page:**
- üü¢ Green (> 3%): Good time to own stocks
- ‚ö™ White (1-3%): Neutral
- üî¥ Red (< 1%): Stocks expensive vs bonds

**Real-world impact:**
- Low premium = people rushing into stocks (maybe too much)
- High premium = stocks unloved (often a buying opportunity)

---

### 5. **Term Premium**
**What it is:** Extra return you get for lending money for 10 years vs 2 years.

**Formula:** 10-Year Yield - 2-Year Yield

**Why it matters:**
- Shows the "yield curve" shape
- Predicts recessions when negative

**How to interpret:**
- **+0.5% to +2%:** Normal (upward sloping curve)
- **0% to +0.5%:** Flattening (economy slowing)
- **Negative:** INVERTED CURVE (recession warning!)

**Real-world impact:**
- Inverted (negative) = **recession in next 12-18 months** (historically very accurate)
- Steep (high positive) = economy expanding
- Affects bank profitability (they borrow short, lend long)

---

### 6. **Credit Risk Premium**
**What it is:** Extra yield corporate bonds pay vs safe government bonds.

**Formula:** Corporate Bond Yield - 10-Year Treasury

**Why it matters:**
- Shows how much risk investors see in companies
- Wider spread = more fear of defaults

**How to interpret:**
- **0.5-1.5%:** Low credit stress (confident economy)
- **1.5-2.5%:** Normal credit spread
- **2.5-4%:** Elevated stress
- **4%+:** Crisis mode (2008 was >6%)

**Real-world impact:**
- High spread = companies pay more to borrow
- High spread = corporate bonds become attractive vs stocks
- Predicts economic trouble when very high

---

### 7. **High Yield Spread**
**What it is:** Extra yield "junk bonds" (risky companies) pay vs Treasuries.

**Why it matters:**
- Shows fear of corporate defaults
- Leading indicator of credit stress

**How to interpret:**
- **2-4%:** Very calm markets
- **4-6%:** Normal conditions
- **6-8%:** Moderate stress
- **8-10%:** High stress
- **10%+:** CRISIS (2008 was 20%+)

**Real-world impact:**
- Spikes before recessions
- Shows if credit markets are freezing up
- Affects small/risky companies' ability to borrow

---

### 8. **Liquidity Premium (TED Spread)**
**What it is:** Difference between what banks charge each other vs Treasury rates.

**Formula:** 3-Month LIBOR - 3-Month Treasury

**Why it matters:**
- Shows if banks trust each other
- Measures "financial plumbing" health

**How to interpret:**
- **< 0.5%:** Banks trust each other (normal)
- **0.5-1%:** Moderate stress
- **1-2%:** High stress
- **2%+:** CRISIS (2008 peaked at 4.6%!)

**Real-world impact:**
- High = financial crisis brewing
- High = credit freezing up
- Banks hoard cash instead of lending

---

## üìà MARKET-DERIVED METRICS (What the Market Expects)

### 9. **Real Yield (10Y TIPS)**
**What it is:** Return you get on inflation-protected bonds - this is your TRUE real return after inflation.

**Why it matters:**
- Shows real (inflation-adjusted) investment returns
- Higher = savers are rewarded
- Negative = your money loses value even in "safe" bonds

**How to interpret:**
- **> 2%:** Strong real returns (great for savers)
- **1-2%:** Moderate real returns
- **0-1%:** Low real returns
- **Negative:** Losing purchasing power (even in "safe" bonds!)

**Real-world impact:**
- Negative = Fed is stimulating (forcing you into risk)
- High positive = Fed is tight (rewards savers)
- Affects retirement planning significantly

---

### 10. **Breakeven Inflation**
**What it is:** What the bond market expects inflation to average over next 10 years.

**Formula:** 10Y Treasury - 10Y TIPS (the market calculates this automatically)

**Why it matters:**
- **This is what investors actually expect** for inflation
- More accurate than economist forecasts usually
- Shows if Fed is credible

**How to interpret:**
- **~2%:** Perfect (Fed's target)
- **2.5-3%:** Slightly above target
- **3%+:** Inflation concerns
- **< 1.5%:** Deflation concerns

**Real-world impact:**
- High = people worried about inflation
- Low = people worried about deflation/recession
- Compares to Fed's 2% target

---

### 11. **Estimated Discount Rate**
**What it is:** The rate you should use to value stocks and companies.

**Formula:** 10-Year Treasury + Equity Risk Premium

**Why it matters:**
- Used in DCF (Discounted Cash Flow) models
- Shows "cost of equity capital"
- Higher = stocks should be worth less

**How to interpret:**
- **7-9%:** Normal discount rate
- **9-11%:** Higher (stocks should be cheaper)
- **< 7%:** Low (stocks can be expensive)

**Real-world impact:**
- This is what Warren Buffett uses to value companies
- Higher rate = lower valuations
- Used by every professional investor

---

## üìä MARKET INDICATORS

### 12. **VIX Index**
**What it is:** "Fear gauge" - measures expected stock market volatility.

**Why it matters:**
- Shows if investors are scared or complacent
- Spikes during crises

**How to interpret:**
- **< 12:** Extreme complacency (maybe too calm)
- **12-20:** Normal/calm markets
- **20-30:** Moderate fear/uncertainty
- **30-40:** High fear
- **40+:** PANIC (2008 hit 89, COVID hit 85)

**Real-world impact:**
- High VIX = market swinging wildly
- Low VIX = smooth sailing (but maybe too smooth)
- Often spikes mark market bottoms

---

### 13. **Corporate Bond Yield**
**What it is:** Average yield on investment-grade corporate bonds.

**Why it matters:**
- Shows cost of borrowing for big companies
- Alternative to stock investing

**How to interpret:**
- **3-5%:** Low yields (easy borrowing)
- **5-7%:** Moderate yields
- **7%+:** High yields (stress or high rates)

**Real-world impact:**
- Compare to stock earnings yield
- Higher = bonds compete with stocks
- Affects Apple, Microsoft, etc. borrowing costs

---

### 14. **S&P 500 Price**
**What it is:** Current level of S&P 500 stock index (reference point).

**Why it matters:**
- Shows overall stock market level
- Used to calculate equity premium

**How to interpret:**
- Just a reference number
- More important: compare to earnings (P/E ratio)
